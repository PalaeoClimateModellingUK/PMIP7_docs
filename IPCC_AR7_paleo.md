## Paleoclimate in the IPCC 7th Assessment Report of Working Group I

Paleoclimate information forms a key component of the IPCC’s assessment of climate change. In AR6, paleoclimate evidence was incorporated across chapters as part of the multiple lines of evidence used to assess climate change. 

In AR7, paleoclimate information will again be incorporated across chapters in the IPCC’s WGI assessments of climate change. Paleoclimate expertise is represented across the IPCC chapter author teams, and a cross-chapter paleoclimate author group has been established to coordinate the integration of paleoclimate information across the AR7 report. 

Information on the IPCC AR7, including chapter outlines and authors, is available [here](https://www.ipcc.ch/assessment-report/ar7/).

### Key insights from paleoclimate information that could contribute to AR7 include

* Abrupt changes and tipping systems, including long-term commitments and timescales of change (ch 8)
* Climate stabilisation under different CO2 levels, including informing overshoot and stabilisation potential futures (ch 9)
* Long-term context for observed and projected climate variability and changes, including global and regional temperature and hydroclimate and modes of variability (ch 2, ch 3, ch 4, ch 5, ch 6, ch 7)
* Whether different components of the Earth system have already moved beyond the range of Holocene/recent natural variability, including aspects such as global temperature, ice volume, AMOC and AOC strength (Ch 2)
* Potential for hydroclimate extremes beyond historical experience, including mega droughts and compound extremes across regions (ch 3, ch7, ch 8, ch10)
* Additional lines of evidence for assessment of climate models, including aspects like Antarctic sea ice, ice sheet loss and climate sensitivity, where validation on observational records is limited, as well as through data–model comparisons and assimilation of paleoclimate data (e.g., improvements in parameterisations informed by paleoclimate constraints)  (ch2, ch3, ch 4, ch5, ch 6, ch 7, ch9)
* Use of paleoclimate information to provide multiple lines of evidence in the assessment of climate change, and key paleoclimate reference periods that inform assessments for future climate change (ch 1)
 
This is not an exhaustive list, and we welcome community input on advances in understanding of paleoclimate information since AR6 that should be considered in AR7.

### How to contribute to the IPCC process

__Publish scientific literature for the assessment:__
It is anticipated that publications to be considered for citation in the IPCC AR7 WGI assessment will need to be submitted by March 2027 and accepted by October 2027 (exact cut off dates still to be confirmed). The cut off dates are strictly enforced to avoid bias in the literature.
 
Work should primarily be published in peer reviewed journals, though other kinds of grey literature may be considered, particularly where these relate to diverse knowledge systems including Indigenous People’s knowledge. The assessment will primarily focus on work published since AR6 (post 2020).

Paleoclimate work that includes numerical information that is useful for evaluation, advances process-based understanding, or draws together multiple lines of evidence to demonstrate robust findings, is particularly helpful for incorporation in the IPCC assessment process. Efforts by the paleoclimate community to develop publications that respond to research gaps highlighted in previous IPCC reports, which flag policy relevant research directions, will also be particularly useful for AR7.

The community may also wish to consider if journal special issues or collections may help to guide researchers in developing literature aligned to the IPCC assessment.

__Bring your work to the attention of IPCC AR7 authors:__

To bring relevant published work to the attention of IPCC AR7 authors, you can:
* Complete this [Google Form](https://docs.google.com/forms/d/e/1FAIpQLSejlgG_-xDmvnZwLcjVaUrZgCR6v_uIqnCvyn1EAzQprT2QzA/viewform) to identify relevant paleoclimate literature and provide a brief perspective on the insights this brings to a particular chapter or topic. 
* Contact relevant chapter authors, alerting them of your work.  Information on the IPCC AR7, including chapter outlines and authors, is available [here](https://www.ipcc.ch/assessment-report/ar7/).
 
__Be part of the IPCC process:__

Information and guidance on how to participate in the IPCC is available here. This includes registering to be an expert reviewer, and coordinating groups of reviewers to review the inclusion of particular themes across the whole report, for the First Order Draft (review period expected 10th August – 2nd October 2026).

## IPCC authors who are familiar with palaeoclimate

* Chapter 1
  * Nerilie Abram (CLA). Her main topic is paleoclimate reconstructions, using both ice-cores and corals. 
  * Ana Maria Duran Quesada. A regional climate modeller. Chris and Ilana once got her involved in a proposal downscaling for the LGM and midHolocene over South America though
  * Gabi Hegerl. She has been involved with PMIP for quite a while, but has moved onto WhatIfMIP recently. She is interested in detection & attribution and volcanoes - so her PMIP stuff has been around the last millennium.
  * Yongqiang Yu. Was involved in PMIP around 15-20 yrs ago. All the FGOALS simulations for PMIP2 were performed (or supervised?) by him

* Chapter 2
  * Luciana Prado. A climate modeller with a fair interest and publiction history in palaeoclimate.
  * David de Vleeschouwer. A palaeoceanographer interested in the long-term evolution of climate (from 60Ma)
  * Wenxia Zhang. She hasn't published on past climates herself, but worked with Tianjun Zhou at CAS (see Chap 6)

* Chapter 5
  * Natalie Mahowald. Has been working with Allegra LeGrande at NASA around water isotopes. This is primarily around the modern system, but she's aware of paleo questions.

* Chapter 6
  * Tamsin Edwards (CLA). An ice-sheet modeller. She has supervised PhD students looking at Pliocene Antarctic ice sheet losses (one of them with me).
  * Tianjun Zhou (CLA). A monsoon specialist in Beijing with a wide range of interests, one of which includes palaeoclimates. Will be arranging for some PMIP simulations in CMIP7, and had this nice Nature paper recently: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-08956-6
  * Josephine Brown. Interested in ENSO and Southern Hemisphere. She wrote the paleoENSO paper in the last PMIP.

* Chapter 7
  * Marcelo Barreiro. Did some work on Pliocene equatorial gradients during his postdoc at Yale.

* Chapter 8 
  * Shipra Jain (CLA). Hasn't published on paleo yet, but has been on a paleo-monsoon proposal with Chris
  * Robert Kopp. Sea level specialist, especially paleo sea levels
  * Tim Lenton. Tipping point specialist. Has been looking at paleo to provide long enough records for tipping point analysis
  * Laurie Menviel. Australia's leader of PMIP. Mainly interglacials
  * Shuting Yang. Ice-sheet modeller - involved with palaeo people coming around tipping points. Works with Qiong Zhang at Stockholm.
