Past climate and environmental records depict a range of climate states, some representing very warm and very cold states (locally and globally) as well as major variations of the hydrological cycles (e.g. monsoon systems), atmospheric dust content (very large during glacial periods), flora and fauna, peatland and permafrost, ice sheets. Understanding all these records in a consistent manner is a challenge for climate system models. Some records, such as the ice core record above, testify of fast (within a decade) climate changes, often related to abrupt changes in other component of the climate system (e.g. ocean overturning circulation)

There is no perfect analogue of future climates, from the forcing point of view, among past climate states. However, these provide stringent tests for our understanding of how the climate system work and how it should be modelled. Different climate models, of different complexities, are used to study the role of different climate system components and different time scales. Climate model hierarchies are very useful for understanding paleoclimate and paleoenvironmental changes.

The evolution of the Earthʼs climate shows large variations, from ice worlds to hot-houses, accompanied by dramatic ecosystem shifts. The Paleoclimate Modelling Intercomparison Project was set up with three objectives: (a) coordinate paleoclimate simulations for key time periods, in order to perform
multi-model, multi-period comparisons giving context to the future; (b) benchmark paleoclimate simulations via paleodata syntheses, so that processes explaining modelled climate differences can be assessed and (c) create a forum for discussing these results.

The results from PMIP have contributed to previous model assessments, as reviewed by all IPCC assessment reports. PMIP started with two periods: the mid-Holocene, ca. 6000 years ago, with an enhanced boreal seasonal cycle and strong Asian and African monsoons, the latter contributing to a greener Sahara; and 2) the Last Glacial Maximum (LGM), ca. 21000 years ago, with large and high ice sheets over northern North America and northwestern Eurasia and lower atmospheric greenhouse gases. Across different generations, models show difficulties in simulating monsoons as strong as recorded for the mid-Holocene and in simulating correct cooling patterns for the LGM. Here, we will describe successes and challenges of past PMIP assessments, as well as the 'abrupt127k' experiment for the AR7 FastTrack exercise, an experiment set up to test the ability of models to simulate the disappearance of Arctic sea ice. At a time when new types of models arise, PMIP offers strong out-of sample tests for these models as it has done for the General Coupled Models for more than 30 years.

Some long lasting data-model discrepancies include modelling the African monsoon during the mid-Holocene and the winter temperatures over western Europe at the LGM.

Using data assimilation is promising (cf. Tierney+ 2020 for the LGM, Jebri & Khodri 2023 for the last millenium) to reconstruct the most physically/biogeochemically plausible earth system, taking the best
from models and data. The resulting state is highly dependent on the chosen data interpretation (cf. uncertainties for the glacial state illustrated in frame 5) and on the model. It should therefore ideally be the topic of further model and methodology comparisons to quantify the uncertainties of the modelled climate state.
